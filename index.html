02

The design loop

A repeatable process for making better system decisions.

  1. 01

    Clarify

    Define the user outcome, contract, constraints, and success.

  2. 02

    Explore

    Generate options and compare their trade-offs.

  3. 03

    Design

    Choose a mechanism and document the rationale.

  4. 04

    Validate

    Attack assumptions with failure and capacity tests.

  5. 05

    Learn

    Observe production and feed evidence back into design.

Seven questions before the first box

  1. What user outcome must succeed?
  2. What must always remain true?
  3. What may be stale, delayed, partial, approximate, or unavailable?
  4. Which state is authoritative?
  5. Which finite resource fails first?
  6. What can happen after a timeout or crash at each boundary?
  7. What evidence would make the central claim credible?

05

Failure-mode matrix

Ask what breaks, how you know, what the user sees, and how you prove recovery.

Traffic spikeLatency, errors, queue growth, and cost

Hidden mechanism: arrival rate exceeds service rate or stateless scaling multiplies downstream pressure.

Safer design: admission control, bounded queues, tenant fairness, backpressure, and a tested recovery curve.

Proof: burst beyond measured capacity with one failure domain absent; resource use must stay bounded.

Dependency slowdownTimeouts, retries, degraded features

Hidden mechanism: budgets do not shrink across hops and retries amplify a saturated dependency.

Safer design: deadlines, one retry owner, jitter, circuit state, and product-defined fallback.

Proof: delay and fail the dependency while checking retry budget, queue age, and user-level success.

Duplicate or reordered workRepeated effects and stale state

Hidden mechanism: delivery is at least once and ordering has an unstated scope.

Safer design: semantic idempotency keys, monotonic versions, deduplication, and explicit ordering boundaries.

Proof: duplicate, delay, and reorder every externally retried operation and broker message.

Stale owner or split brainConflicting writes and corruption

Hidden mechanism: detecting failure or electing a leader does not revoke the old actor.

Safer design: ownership epochs and fencing checked by the protected resource.

Proof: pause the owner beyond lease expiry, start a new owner, then resume the stale process.

Data corruption or partial publicationIncorrect results and loss of trust

Hidden mechanism: a file, index, model, or configuration becomes visible before validation completes.

Safer design: immutable versioned builds, checksums, atomic publication, and retained rollback.

Proof: interrupt every publication step and verify readers see either the old complete version or the new complete version.

Disk, memory, or connection saturationProcess failure and cascading overload

Hidden mechanism: logical accounting misses RSS, per-process pools multiply, or buffers are unbounded.

Safer design: measure the actual scarce resource and define soft limits, hard limits, rejection, and recovery.

Proof: constrain each resource independently, then combine faults while preserving the control plane.

Bad deploy or schema migrationMixed versions and failed rollback

Hidden mechanism: code, data, and configuration compatibility were changed in one irreversible step.

Safer design: expand, migrate, verify, contract; canary; immutable artifacts; delayed cleanup.

Proof: run old and new readers together, stop and resume backfill, and roll back before cleanup.

Region or control-plane lossPartial availability and stale authority

Hidden mechanism: the recovery region still depends on identity, keys, queues, artifacts, or capacity in the failed region.

Safer design: dependency inventory, fenced ownership, measured RPO and RTO, reconciliation, and failback.

Proof: an authorized game day must shift traffic, validate authority, measure data loss, and safely fail back.

Safety boundary: destructive tests belong in disposable environments or explicitly authorized game days with a bounded target, abort condition, rollback, monitoring, and operator kill switch.

Interview practice plan

A four-week path that builds judgment, not memorized diagrams.

Week 01

Contract & numbers

Requirements, non-goals, SLOs, invariants, workload, and the smallest credible design.

  • Four timed designs
  • Invariant table
  • Capacity worksheet
Week 02

Data & async work

Transactions, isolation, caches, idempotency, outbox, ordering, and job state.

  • Crash-point diagrams
  • Replay safety
  • Authoritative-state review
Week 03

Distribution & resilience

Partitioning, migration, fencing, overload, degradation, recovery, and region failure.

  • Failure matrix
  • Migration state machine
  • Recovery proof
Week 04

Operations & execution

Observability, rollout, internals, incident learning, and three no-notes mock interviews.

  • Mock reviews
  • One polished design
  • One-page checklist

08

Software engineer project ladder

Build projects that force the next layer of engineering judgment.

  1. Level 1

    One correct service

    API contracts, validation, SQL, transactions, tests, telemetry, deploy, and rollback.

    Proof: invariants covered and rollback rehearsed.
  2. Level 2

    Reliable async work

    Queues, retries, idempotency, outbox, dead letters, backfills, and job state machines.

    Proof: kill-and-replay convergence.
  3. Level 3

    Storage & finite resources

    Indexes, caching, persistence, load, saturation, admission control, fairness, and recovery.

    Proof: first SLO breach and recovery curve measured.
  4. Level 4

    Distributed control

    Replication, leases, fencing, shard ownership, live migration, and immutable publication.

    Proof: failover or migration rehearsal with rollback.
  5. Level 5

    Production ownership

    SLOs, alerts, traces, canaries, incidents, restore drills, cost, security, and on-call.

    Proof: an incident changes and verifies a design control.
  6. Level 6

    Staff-level judgment

    Simplification, platform contracts, risk portfolios, build versus buy, and multi-quarter change.

    Proof: a decision record with evidence and revisit trigger.
